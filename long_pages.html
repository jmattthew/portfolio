1. Long pages may load faster than many short pages,
and they let users to see what lies ahead. Clicking feels
like commitment, while scrolling feels like exploring
Why would users prefer a long page?
2. Scrolling is easier than clicking because it doesn’t
require precise hand movement or visual targeting.
Plus, you can scroll wherever your hand is now,
with keyboard, scroll-wheel, or trackpad, or finger
3. Scrolling is instant and predictable: it always moves
the page up & down. Clicking is unpredictable: it may
load a new page, hide useful content, create a pop-up,
or cause an unexpected delay
● Goals that have multiple steps, such as multipart form,
work best as one page because users want to see what
lies ahead and review everything they have input
● Marketing pages - home pages and lists of services,
features, or product categories. These are launching
pads that users want to explore with scrolling
When are long pages a best practice?
learn more
get started
get a quote
● Single-column forms are easier to complete, even
though they make the page longer. Like bullet points,
text fields and radio options are easier to read when
stacked vertically. Users don’t like to move their
mouse back & forth horizontally
● Walls of text are intimidating for users. Long text
(e.g. FAQs) can be broken across multiple pages or
hidden within expanding/collapsing sections
● Fast loading pages are critical. If a long page
loads too slowly, it should be optimized or split
● No best practice applies to every situation.
If a shorter page wins an A/B test, trust your data.
